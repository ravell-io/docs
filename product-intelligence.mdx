Ravell doesn't just answer your questions — it proactively surfaces what matters. **Product intelligence** builds a structured product graph from your connected sources, tracking problems, features, decisions, and product areas. It then scores, ranks, and delivers insights so you can focus on the right things.

---

## What the product graph tracks

The product graph is a layer on top of Ravell's [knowledge graph](/knowledge-graph) that identifies and connects product-level concepts:

| Entity type | What it represents | Example |
|-------------|-------------------|---------|
| **Problem** | A customer pain point, bug, or issue | "Checkout timeout errors", "Slow dashboard load" |
| **Feature** | A product capability or feature request | "Bulk export", "SSO support" |
| **Decision** | A product decision that affects problems or features | "Deprioritize PDF export", "Resolve billing bugs in Q2" |
| **Product area** | A broad area of the product | "Billing", "Onboarding", "API" |

These entities are extracted from your connected sources (Linear issues, Notion pages, Intercom conversations, Slack messages) using both metadata and AI-powered extraction. When a Linear issue is labeled "bug", Ravell creates a problem entity. When an Intercom conversation and a Linear issue both mention the same pain point, Ravell links them.

---

## Assertions and evidence

Every connection in the product graph is backed by an **assertion** — a claim that two entities are related, grounded in a specific document.

For example:
- "Checkout timeout errors" **is evidenced by** a specific Intercom conversation
- "SSO support" **addresses** the problem "Enterprise customers blocked on evaluation"
- "Billing" **contains** the feature "Subscription management"

Each assertion carries a **confidence score** (0–1) and tracks where it was extracted from. Higher-confidence assertions from structured metadata (like Linear labels) are automatically promoted, while lower-confidence ones from AI extraction stay in review.

## Ranked problems

Ravell scores every problem entity using four signals:

| Signal | Weight | What it measures |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| **Evidence volume** | 30% | How many documents mention this problem (log-scaled) |
| **Confidence** | 25% | How strong the evidence is across assertions |
| **Recency** | 25% | How recently the problem was mentioned |
| **Source diversity** | 20% | How many different tools report this problem |

A problem mentioned once in a single Slack thread scores lower than one referenced across Linear issues, Intercom conversations, and Notion docs. Recency ensures stale problems naturally decay over time.

Problems are also tagged with **coverage status** — whether any feature or decision in the graph addresses them. Uncovered problems with high scores are your biggest gaps.

## Blind spots

Blind spot detection finds problems that are rising in mentions but lack sufficient attention. Ravell flags three types:

- **New** — A problem entity that appeared recently with significant evidence
- **Rising** — A problem whose mention velocity is increasing (2x or more compared to baseline)
- **Uncovered** — A frequently mentioned problem with no linked feature or decision addressing it

Each blind spot includes a severity score so you can prioritize which ones to investigate first.

## Weekly brief

The **weekly product intelligence brief** is a scheduled report that summarizes the state of your product graph. It runs automatically (default: Monday at 9am) and delivers to your Inbox and optionally to a Slack channel.

Each brief includes:

| Section | What it shows |
|---------|---------------|
| **Rising** | Problems with accelerating evidence velocity |
| **Blind spots** | New, rising, or uncovered issues that need attention |
| **Improving** | Problems where evidence volume dropped after a linked feature shipped |
| **Changes since last brief** | New entries, rank changes, and dropped items compared to the previous week |
| **Possible duplicates** | Entity pairs with high semantic similarity that may represent the same thing |
| **Graph health** | Total entities, assertions, promotion rate, and staleness percentage |

### Enable the weekly brief

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open product intelligence">
    Navigate to **Product Intelligence** in the dashboard.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Enable the brief">
    Click **Enable weekly brief**. Ravell creates a scheduled automation that runs every Monday at 9am.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Choose a Slack channel (optional)">
    If Slack is connected, select a channel to receive the brief. You can change this later.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Customize the schedule">
    Adjust the day, time, and timezone to match your team's cadence.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
You can test the brief before waiting for the next scheduled run. Use **Send test brief** to deliver a one-time brief to your selected Slack channel.
</Tip>

### Pause or disable

You can pause the weekly brief at any time from the product intelligence settings. Pausing stops scheduled runs but preserves your configuration. Re-enable to resume.

## How extraction works

Ravell builds the product graph using two extraction methods:

1. **Metadata extraction** — Structured data from your tools. For example, a Linear issue labeled "bug" automatically creates a problem entity. A Linear project creates a product area entity. These assertions have high confidence and are promoted automatically.

2. **AI extraction** — Ravell reads document content and identifies product entities and their relationships. This catches mentions that metadata alone would miss — like a customer describing a pain point in an Intercom conversation, or a decision documented in a Notion page. AI-extracted assertions start at lower confidence and are promoted when corroborated by additional evidence.

Both methods contribute to the same graph. Over time, entities that appear across multiple sources and extraction methods build stronger evidence and higher confidence scores.

---

## Velocity tracking

Ravell tracks how frequently each problem is mentioned over time. It compares the last 14 days against a 76-day baseline to compute a **velocity ratio**:

- **Rising** (2x+ baseline) — This problem is accelerating and may need attention
- **Steady** (0.5x–2x baseline) — Mention rate is stable
- **Declining** (below 0.5x baseline) — This problem is fading

Velocity data feeds into the weekly brief's "Rising" section and the problem priority score's recency signal.

---

## Impact detection

When a feature ships and a linked problem's evidence volume drops, Ravell detects the impact. It compares the 30-day window before and after a feature's most recent activity to measure the change.

Problems that show a meaningful decline in evidence after a feature shipped appear in the brief's "Improving" section — giving your team visibility into what's working.
